# Writing Skill: Articles and Posts

## Purpose

This system is for writing that explores an idea in public.

Optimized for:

- Reframing a problem
- Thinking in public
- Building a reader's mental model

Not optimized for:

- Step by step instruction
- Decision memos
- Execution plans

When the goal is instruction or execution, relax the anti-closure bias on purpose.

## Core principle

Every unit has a job.

- Every sentence has a goal.
- Every paragraph has a goal.
- Every technique has a goal.

Optimize around those goals. Do not optimize around the rubric.

## Operating stance

The guide exists to remove failure modes.

Stop using it when it turns into compliance.

Break rules on purpose when you can name the failure mode the rule prevents.

## Vocabulary

**Piece**: the draft you are editing.

**Section**: a block under a heading.

**Skeleton**: the outline of headings plus the first sentence of each paragraph.

**Border**: the join between two adjacent sections.

**Tempo**: the pattern of sentence and paragraph lengths, plus any repeated rhetorical moves that create rhythm.

## Non negotiables

The items in this section are hard bans. One instance fails the piece.

### Banned punctuation and openings

- No em dash characters.
- No sentences that start with these words: But, And, So, Or.
- No sentences that start with these substitutes: Still, Yet, However, Therefore.

Allowed: those words in the middle of a sentence.

### Banned list shaped prose

- No triads in prose.
- No pseudo lists made from parallel clauses.

Common forms that fail:

- Do X, do Y, do Z.
- How X works, how Y works, how Z works.
- Where A happens, where B happens, where C happens.
- That does X, that does Y, that does Z.
- Every A, every B, every C.

Fixes that pass:

- Join two items with "and" and drop the third.
- Expand one item into a sentence with a different shape.
- Split into two sentences with unequal weight.

### Banned cadence templates

- No three consecutive sentences with the same opening word.
- No three consecutive sentences with the same grammatical frame.

Examples that usually fail:

- Three sentences that start with If.
- Three sentences that start with This.
- Find X. Find Y. Find Z.

### Banned hedging

No vague modal hedges:

```
might
could
perhaps
```

If a claim is conditional, state the condition.

```
Bad: This might improve performance.
Good: This improves performance when latency dominates throughput.
```

### Banned filler and posturing

- No throat clearing phrases.
- No cliches unless the piece is clearly subverting them.
- No moralizing or status language.

Cut phrases like:

```
it's worth noting that
the question becomes
one of the few ways
something like
```

Cut frames like:

```
Everyone gets this wrong.
People do not realize.
The real truth is.
```

Replace with personal discovery or direct observation.

### Banned performative intent

Remove sentences that exist to demonstrate insight, summarize, or land the point.

Keep sentences that exist because the thought exists.

## Rationed patterns

These are allowed, with budgets.

The budgets are defaults. A long piece can sustain more than a short piece.

- Staccato paragraph closers: one, maybe two.
- Parallel paragraph openings: rare.
- Imperative chains: one per piece.
- Contrast pairs: use them as a tool, not as a metronome.
- Rhetorical question followed by an answer: one per piece.

Spacing matters as much as count.

Back to back uses read as a tic.

Even spacing across the whole piece reads as a template.

## Rubrics

Apply these in order. All must pass.

### Rubric 1: Story beats

The piece should feel like a journey, not a report.

Required beats:

1. A hook that creates tension.
2. A reframe that explains the hook.
3. A problem the reader can feel.
4. A concrete anchor before abstraction.
5. A hypothesis stated with confidence when it arrives.
6. A walk through the reasoning.
7. A lens shift that changes how the reader sees the problem.
8. Sections that each do one job.
9. A hidden skeleton.

Hidden skeleton rule:

- Headings name content, not rhetorical function.
- Headings like Problem, Solution, Conclusion fail.

### Intro structure

The intro is the hypothesis approached from multiple angles.

Moves to cover, in any order:

- Tension hook
- Immediate reframe
- Problem and first pass hypothesis
- Acknowledge confusion
- Concrete hypothesis

The hook and reframe must point at the same object as the hypothesis.

If you can label the intro beats while reading, the structure is showing.

Compress or scramble.

### Rubric 2: AI tell filter

Run the hard bans first.

Then remove softer tells.

#### Soft tells to remove

- Comma heavy sentences that read like lists.
- Repeated sentence structures even when count is under three.
- Overuse of parallel structure.
- Default negative language.

Negatives are allowed when they exclude a plausible misreading.

Negatives fail when they posture.

#### Closure signals

Avoid forms that package the thought as finished.

Neat takeaways, balanced contrasts, and smooth summaries often signal closure.

Use closure only when you want the reader to stop thinking.

### Rubric 3: Flow and cohesion

This rubric is about joins.

#### Section borders

For every section border, the reader should know why the piece is moving.

Add a bridge when needed.

Bridges that often work:

- A short question that opens the next move.
- A sentence that names the next constraint.

#### Paragraph cohesion

Each paragraph needs unity and a topic sentence.

Each paragraph should develop one idea.

Use the known to new pattern inside paragraphs:

- Start sentences with familiar information.
- End sentences with new information.

If the paragraph feels jumpy, rewrite sentence starts to carry the topic forward.

#### Causality

For each major claim, write a one sentence because.

If the because is hand wavy, supply the missing link or narrow the claim.

#### Cognitive load

Keep the reader oriented.

When the piece introduces many new ideas in a row, insert a sentence that cashes out implications.

Prefer concrete before abstract.

### Rubric 4: Adversarial robustness

A piece can pass the first three rubrics and still fail with a skeptical reader.

#### Intent alignment

Write two private sentences:

- If this succeeds, what will the reader think differently.
- What should the reader not conclude.

Do not publish these sentences.

Use them to discipline scope and phrasing.

#### Pushback pass

Name the strongest objection a knowledgeable skeptic would raise.

Choose one:

- Make the objection impossible.
- Make it irrelevant.
- Narrow the claim so the objection no longer applies.

#### Worst case reader test

Identify the most likely low context or bad faith reader.

Write the accusation they would make.

Choose one:

- Preempt the misreading.
- Accept it.
- Sharpen the post so the reaction is unavoidable but contained.

#### Quote test

Pull three sentences at random.

Read them as if quoted by someone hostile.

Revise any sentence that can be used against the intent.

#### Evidence toggle

If the piece makes empirical claims:

- cite, or
- label as hypothesis, or
- give a concrete anecdote and label it as anecdote.

## Tempo guidance

Tempo creates emphasis.

Short sentences land.

Long sentences connect.

Variation matters more than any single choice.

Failure modes:

- Metronomic cadence: similar sentence lengths for long stretches.
- Clustered rhythm devices: contrast pairs, staccato closers, repeated openings packed together.

A simple test:

Read the piece aloud.

Mark where your attention drifts.

Fix those spots.

## Process

### Step 0: Capture

Start from real material.

Write notes without an audience.

Edit captured thinking into prose.

### Step 1: Draft

Write fast.

Do not run rubrics during drafting.

### Step 2: Extract skeleton

Produce:

- The heading outline
- The first sentence of each paragraph
- A border list that pairs the last sentence of a section with the first sentence of the next

Use the skeleton to spot:

- visible templates
- missing steps
- weak borders

### Step 3: Rubric passes

Run rubrics in order.

Fix failures.

Restart from Rubric 1 after major edits.

### Step 4: Final read

Read as a reader.

Do not polish voice out of the piece.

## Output requirements for an editing agent

When an agent edits a piece, it returns two artifacts:

1. The revised piece.
2. A short report that lists:
   - which rubric failures were found
   - what was changed
   - which risks remain

The report should name concrete examples, not vibes.

## Example transformation

Use examples as illustration, not as a template.

```
AI-style:
There are three key benefits to this approach. First, it is more efficient. Second, it is more scalable. Third, it is more maintainable. These benefits combine to create a compelling case for adoption.

Revised:
It is faster and scales better. Maintenance is where it matters. When something breaks, you can find the problem without rebuilding the whole system.
```

What changed:

- The list packaging is gone.
- One point gets the weight.
- A concrete reason shows up.
- The paragraph does not summarize itself.

## Voice and tone

Tone is the local mood.

Voice is the long-run signature.

Tone changes with context. Voice stays stable across pieces.

Voice shows up in:

- sentence rhythm preferences
- vocabulary range
- distance from the reader
- how uncertainty is handled
- what the writer finds funny

Voice is not constructed from scratch. Editing can erase it.

A practical guard:

Read the draft aloud.

When a sentence feels correct but not like you, rewrite until it sounds like you again.

## Compression and expansion

Density is a choice.

Compress when:

- the reader already has context
- the point is simple
- you are building momentum

Expand when:

- the idea is unfamiliar
- the logic has steps that cannot be skipped
- misunderstanding is likely

Failure modes:

- compression that loses the reader
- expansion that loses attention

## Abstraction laddering

A piece moves between concrete and abstract.

Levels:

- Concrete: named situations, specific examples, particular details
- Pattern: categories, mechanisms, comparisons
- Principle: general constraints and claims

Guideline:

Ground the reader before you climb.

Better than a single climb is a series of moves:

- ladder up when the pattern is almost obvious
- ladder down when the claim becomes floaty

A common tic:

Ending every section with a general claim.

Let some sections end concrete.

## Distribution patterns

Avoid repetition at three levels:

- the pattern itself
- the spacing between instances
- the shape of the distribution across the whole piece

A simple mapping trick:

Mark where you used any rationed pattern.

If the map has a rhythm, break the rhythm.

## Audience calibration

Assume a smart reader. Calibrate for their context.

Key axes:

- technical vs general
- sympathetic vs hostile
- peers vs public

Calibration is not pandering.

It is choosing what work the reader can do.

## Knowing when a piece is done

A piece is done when further revision trades off against itself.

Signals you are done:

- edits get smaller
- you revert changes from earlier passes
- the piece sounds like you when read cold

Signals you are not done:

- a section feels wrong and you avoid it
- the rubrics fail
- quotes misrepresent the intent

## Idea selection

Not every thought is ready to publish.

Ready:

- a clear hypothesis in one sentence
- a real counterargument exists in your head
- the thinking produced at least one surprise

Not ready:

- the hypothesis is only "X is interesting"
- the counterargument is unknown
- the draft is all framing and no constraint

## Final check

Before publishing:

1. Does the hook create tension tied to the hypothesis.
2. Is the problem felt, not only stated.
3. Does every section earn its place.
4. Are borders invisible.
5. Does the ending resonate with the opening.
6. Does the piece survive the quote test.
7. What would a hostile reader accuse it of saying.
8. Which sentence feels most like it was written to impress.

## Piece type adaptations

### Pitch deck (public buy-in)

Piece type: persuasive pitch with case study elements.

Adjustments from defaults:

- Story beats: hook is the reader's daily pain, not an abstract tension. Reframe is "this is a system problem, not a tooling problem."
- Closure: allowed at section boundaries (each section resolves one concern). Final section stays open (call to action, not conclusion).
- Evidence toggle: cite market research for competitor claims, label roadmap items as committed plans, label future capabilities as "planned for Phase N."
- Audience calibration: guild officers who manage 50-200 member gaming communities. Technical enough to understand operational concepts. Not engineers.
- Adversarial robustness: the hostile reader is an officer who has been burned by bots that overpromise and underdeliver. The quote test matters.

### Executive roadmap (internal buy-in)

Piece type: whitepaper/decision memo.

Adjustments from defaults:

- Story beats: the hook is the strategic opportunity. The reframe is "value ordering reveals what to build first." Lens shift is optional.
- Closure: is a feature. Each phase should feel decided. The reader wants to know what was chosen and why.
- Evidence toggle: every value claim cites market research. Every dependency cites the proposal. Evidence density is high.
- Audience calibration: project contributors, potential collaborators, and the project owner. Technical and strategic. Expects rigor.
- Adversarial robustness: the skeptic asks "why this ordering and not another?" Every phase ordering must survive that question.
- Hidden skeleton: relaxed. Headings can be structural (Phase 1, Phase 2) because the audience expects it.
