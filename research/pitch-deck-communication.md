# Pitch Deck Communication Research

## What Investors Want to See

- There is strong overlap across YC and Sequoia on the core deck structure. The standard spine is: company purpose/title, problem, solution, traction, market, competition/alternatives, business model, team, vision/why now, and ask/use of funds. Sequoia explicitly lists company purpose, problem, solution, why now, market potential, competition/alternatives, business model, team, financials, and vision. YC's seed and Series A templates cover the same core sequence, with the ask/use of funds as the end of the story.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/2u-how-to-build-your-seed-round-pitch-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/

- The main deck should stay concise. YC recommends building a Series A deck from 10-15 bullet points/slides, excluding the appendix, and keeping the spoken pitch to roughly 15-20 minutes to leave room for discussion. YC's design guidance also says people only remember a few ideas, so each slide should communicate one idea clearly and immediately.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck

- Appendix material belongs outside the core narrative. YC says the appendix should hold Q&A ammunition such as financial projections, detailed use of funds, and answers to likely investor questions. The main deck should give a clear overview and preserve room for deeper questions rather than trying to download everything at once.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- What makes a deck stand out is not novelty of format but clarity, narrative discipline, and credibility. YC says investors should be able to glance at each slide and immediately understand the point. First Round says answers should be concise, data-backed, and built around the investor's "burning questions." Norwest says founders often lose credibility by failing to address the actual risk areas investors care about.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/

- What gets a deck ignored is usually a credibility problem, not a formatting problem. Repeated warnings across sources include: vanity metrics, vague market sizing, pretending there is no competition, asking for capital without a deployment plan, overselling traction, and hiding unresolved issues.
  Sources:
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/

- Slide-count guidance is stage- and context-sensitive. YC's oral pitch guidance points toward a 10-15 slide core deck, while DocSend's deck analysis says older winning decks would need more category coverage today and references a 20-slide benchmark in its analyzer. The practical synthesis is: keep the main story to roughly 10-15 purposeful slides, allow up to about 20 for an email/read-through deck if every slide earns its place, and push detail into the appendix.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://www.docsend.com/blog/pitch-deck-examples/

## Market Opportunity Communication

- Investors prefer bottom-up market sizing, especially for niche or new-category products. YC explicitly recommends calculating market size as the number of prospective customers multiplied by the value of each customer to you, using real customer segments and current value rather than broad report-based top-down numbers.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck

- "Why now?" is part of market communication, not a decorative slide. Sequoia treats it as a core section and asks why the solution did not emerge earlier. First Round's nonobvious-markets guidance says timing usually comes from changing dynamics such as new technology, falling costs, new distribution, or a changed customer environment.
  Sources:
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/
  https://review.firstround.com/future-founders-heres-how-to-spot-and-build-in-nonobvious-markets/

- A niche wedge is not a fatal problem if it credibly expands. First Round argues that many apparently "small" markets are really entry wedges, and explicitly notes that market entry strategy is often different from market disruption strategy. Their advice is to avoid confusing "niche" with "boring," "high-end," or "personally unfamiliar."
  Sources:
  https://review.firstround.com/future-founders-heres-how-to-spot-and-build-in-nonobvious-markets/

- Investors are skeptical of unsophisticated TAM claims. First Round calls out founders who throw around giant market numbers without reflecting their near-term market, and YC similarly warns against top-down numbers that are not specific to the actual segment the company serves.
  Sources:
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck

- Inference for this project: the deck should not frame the opportunity as "some fraction of all Discord servers." A more credible framing is a bottoms-up wedge: serious gaming guilds and Discord-native communities with officer/admin overhead, then expansion into adjacent operational communities such as creator teams, mod teams, fandom communities, esports organizations, and other coordination-heavy groups. The market story should show an entry wedge, not a universal claim.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/future-founders-heres-how-to-spot-and-build-in-nonobvious-markets/

- Inference for this project: the "why now" argument should probably tie together Discord's maturity as an operating surface, increasing complexity of community operations, the fragmentation of bot-based tooling, and the availability of workflow automation/OCR/AI that can reduce officer labor. That is the style of timing narrative Sequoia and First Round are asking for.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/
  https://review.firstround.com/future-founders-heres-how-to-spot-and-build-in-nonobvious-markets/

## Risk Communication

- Investors want risk surfaced, not hidden. First Round recommends explicitly listing the "burning questions" investors will ask, and says credibility increases when founders get ahead of what they have not figured out yet. Norwest likewise says many founders fail because they do not acknowledge the critical risk areas VCs are evaluating.
  Sources:
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/

- Common risk categories are consistent across sources: founder/CEO risk, team risk, market risk, product/technology risk, customer acquisition risk, competition risk, financing/runway risk, and exit/outcome risk.
  Sources:
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/

- Risk should be framed as managed and measurable, not hand-waved. a16z advises founders to ask themselves difficult questions before investors do, especially around retention, CAC, burn, and operational weak points. First Round similarly argues that overselling or spinning issues destroys trust.
  Sources:
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- A credible mitigation strategy is specific. a16z says use of proceeds must connect to the highest-ROI levers and concrete milestones, not generic spending buckets. YC's ask slide similarly says the deck should show what the capital gets you over the next 18-24 months.
  Sources:
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck

- Practical implication: a founder does not need a giant "risk factors" dump in the main story, but the deck should make the biggest risks legible somewhere obvious. The best pattern from these sources is to integrate risk ownership into traction, competition, use of funds, and appendix Q&A, with a concise risk/mitigation summary if needed.
  Sources:
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/

- Inference for this project: the key disclosed risks are likely market-adoption risk, execution risk, incumbent-bot response risk, and platform/technical dependency risk. Each should be paired with a concrete mitigation, for example pilot guilds and operational proof for adoption risk, phased scope for execution risk, deeper workflow/data moat for incumbent response risk, and API/platform abstraction or fallback ops for technical dependency risk.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

## Financial and Cost Communication

- Investors still expect financial clarity before revenue. OpenVC says that early-stage founders without revenue should focus on unit economics and financial KPIs such as burn rate, runway, CAC, and LTV to show they understand how the business will make and spend money. Sequoia and YC both include business model and financials as standard components even at early stages.
  Sources:
  https://www.openvc.app/blog/financials-slide
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/2u-how-to-build-your-seed-round-pitch-deck

- Use-of-funds communication matters as much as topline projections. YC says the ask slide should show how much money you need, what you will use it for, and what milestone it gets you to. a16z says founders need to be deliberate and precise about use of proceeds and show why those allocations will actually move the business.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/

- Burn rate and runway should be explicit, not implied. Visible defines runway as starting cash divided by monthly net burn rate and recommends founders track it closely; it also gives a general rule of thumb that seed and Series A companies often target 12-18 months of runway. OpenVC says runway and burn rate are essential metrics on a financial slide.
  Sources:
  https://visible.vc/blog/how-to-calculate-runway/
  https://www.openvc.app/blog/financials-slide

- Financial projections need to be grounded, not maximalist. OpenVC says investors do not expect projections to be perfect, but they do expect assumptions to be realistic and tied to how the business actually scales. First Round warns that asking for capital without clearly backing into amount, timeframe, and deployment strategy hurts credibility.
  Sources:
  https://www.openvc.app/blog/financials-slide
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- a16z's down-market guidance is especially relevant for cost communication: founders should stress-test burn multiple, know where the burn is going, and maintain scenario plans rather than assuming one financial path. That is useful even for early projects because it shows discipline under uncertainty.
  Sources:
  https://a16z.com/a-framework-for-navigating-down-markets/
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/

- Inference for this project: infrastructure costs such as Vultr Kubernetes, storage, OCR/API spend, and monitoring should be grouped into a few major cost buckets rather than itemized exhaustively in the main deck. The main deck should show the cost structure, monthly burn, runway under the raise, and what milestone each spending bucket unlocks. Detailed vendor math belongs in the appendix.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://www.openvc.app/blog/financials-slide
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/

- Inference for this project: if revenue is not live yet, the deck should present monetization as a staged model rather than a fully proven business. That likely means showing the likely customer, the likely charging surface, and the milestone that proves willingness to pay. This is more credible than pretending monetization is already validated.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://www.openvc.app/blog/financials-slide
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/2u-how-to-build-your-seed-round-pitch-deck

## Competitive Positioning

- Investors expect a competition slide even when there is no obvious direct rival. Sequoia explicitly asks for competition/alternatives. Norwest says claiming "there is no competition" creates skepticism and founders should discuss substitutes and indirect competitors instead.
  Sources:
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/

- A good competition slide is about plan-to-win, not about listing logos. YC says the purpose is to show why you are 10x better and defensible. First Round criticizes generic x-y logo plots without nuanced explanation of how the company differs.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- Competition should be expressed in buyer-relevant terms. DocSend repeatedly critiques decks that mention competitors without clearly identifying differentiators, and says investors now expect slides such as "Why now," team, and explicit competitive differentiation rather than sparse category claims.
  Sources:
  https://www.docsend.com/blog/pitch-deck-examples/

- Inference for this project: "no direct competitor" is the wrong framing. A more credible framing is "fragmented alternatives plus status quo," such as manual officer workflows, generic Discord bots, spreadsheets, ticket tools, form tools, and internal glue. The deck should compare against those alternatives on the criteria officers actually buy against: workflow depth, auditability, application/ticket support, role automation, cross-surface coordination, and data continuity.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- Inference for this project: the answer to "what if MEE6 or Carl-bot adds this?" should be a moat argument, not a feature argument. The credible position is that a general bot can copy isolated features, but it is harder to copy a system-of-record workflow spanning web intake, Discord actions, role/ticket state, officer review, audit trails, and domain-specific community operations. That is the type of defensibility YC asks the competition slide to communicate.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/

## Writing and Design Style

- The tone that works is clear, concrete, and grounded. YC repeatedly emphasizes plain English, minimal jargon, and slides that are legible, simple, and obvious. First Round argues that founders need to operate at the right "oxygen level" between the weeds and the clouds.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- Storytelling should lead, with data as proof. YC's Series A process explicitly starts from a short narrative list, then attaches evidence under each point. First Round describes the winning pitch as a grand story grounded in the juxtaposition of problem and solution, not in weeds or hand-wavy abstraction.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- Confidence works best when paired with candor. First Round recommends "unapologetic confidence" while also saying that founder credibility rises when founders are open about what they have not solved yet. Norwest likewise says founders need to show they know the risks, not pretend there are none.
  Sources:
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/

- Visual and formatting guidance for a text-based deck still maps cleanly from YC's slide advice: one idea per section, a headline that states the conclusion, high-contrast charts that can be understood at a glance, minimal screenshots, minimal diagrams, and very little decorative filler.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck

- Common mistakes that kill decks: illegible or overloaded slides, subtle points that require interpretation, diagrams that take too long to decode, too many transitions, screenshots that do not explain value, excessive branding, and any metric presentation that feels manipulative or unclear.
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

## Open Source / Community Project Patterns

- Community and open-source pitches are still pitches, but the audience calculus changes. Open Source Guides says a convincing funding case needs to answer: impact, traction, value to funder, use of funds, and how funds will be received. That is much closer to a sponsor/grant/community ask than a traditional venture-return ask.
  Sources:
  https://opensource.guide/getting-paid/

- When the "investment" is contributor time, the pitch has to explain what the project does, why it matters, and why contributors should care. Open Source Guides says maintainers should write down the project's vision, roadmap, expectations, and public processes; this reduces scope confusion and makes it easier for people to pitch in.
  Sources:
  https://opensource.guide/best-practices/
  https://opensource.guide/finding-users/

- Community projects grow by message clarity and active distribution, not by passive discovery. Open Source Guides advises maintainers to explain what the project does and why it matters, go where the audience already is, ask for feedback, and make it easy to find/follow the project.
  Sources:
  https://opensource.guide/finding-users/

- Sponsorship works better when value is packaged clearly. GitHub's 2025 funding analysis says successful projects tend to have engaged communities, solve specific pain points, clearly explain what sponsors get, and make sponsorship visible. Sponsorship is not just "donate because this is nice"; it is often framed as support, priority, recognition, access, or mission alignment.
  Sources:
  https://github.blog/open-source/maintainers/4-trends-shaping-open-source-funding-and-what-they-mean-for-maintainers/

- Organizational backers care about strategic influence, not only altruism. Linux Foundation's strategy guide says organizations invest in open source to influence roadmap, attract contributors, build differentiation, improve efficiency, and reduce risk. That matters for a community platform because time or money from backers may be justified by governance influence and operational leverage, not only financial upside.
  Sources:
  https://www.linuxfoundation.org/resources/open-source-guides/setting-an-open-source-strategy

- Inference for this project: if the deck is meant to recruit contributors or community sponsors, it should not be a watered-down investor deck. It should emphasize impact on the community, proof of demand from officers/admins, a public roadmap, contribution entry points, governance expectations, and exactly what sponsor money or contributor hours unlock.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://opensource.guide/getting-paid/
  https://opensource.guide/best-practices/
  https://github.blog/open-source/maintainers/4-trends-shaping-open-source-funding-and-what-they-mean-for-maintainers/

## Synthesis: Requirements for Our Pitch Deck

- The current deck already covers problem framing, workflows, differentiators, and roadmap. Based on the research, it still needs explicit investor/backer sections for:
  - Why now
  - Market opportunity with a bottoms-up TAM/SAM/SOM or equivalent wedge-expansion framing
  - Business model or funding model
  - Traction or proof proxies
  - Team / why us
  - Ask / use of funds / milestone plan / runway
  - Explicit competitive positioning against substitutes and incumbents
  - Risk ownership and mitigation
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/2u-how-to-build-your-seed-round-pitch-deck
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/

- The market slide should be rebuilt around a bottom-up niche wedge, not a broad top-down "Discord market" number. It should show:
  - Who specifically buys first
  - What they spend today in time or money
  - Why the first wedge is painful enough to support adoption
  - What adjacent customer groups expand the opportunity after the wedge
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://review.firstround.com/future-founders-heres-how-to-spot-and-build-in-nonobvious-markets/

- The competition slide should be repositioned away from "no competitor" language. It should compare against:
  - Manual status quo
  - Generic Discord bots
  - Point tools for forms/tickets/spreadsheets
  - Potential incumbent feature overlap
  It should then argue why an integrated guild-operations system is harder to copy than an isolated bot feature.
  Sources:
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck

- The financial section should be honest about stage. If there is no revenue yet, it should still show:
  - Current and projected monthly burn
  - Runway under the proposed raise
  - Major cost buckets
  - Planned monetization path or support model
  - What milestone proves the next de-risking event
  Sources:
  https://www.openvc.app/blog/financials-slide
  https://visible.vc/blog/how-to-calculate-runway/
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck

- The risk section should be explicit enough to build trust. For this project, the deck should likely name the top 3-4 risks and pair each with a mitigation plan and a milestone that demonstrates progress.
  Sources:
  https://www.norwest.com/blog/how-vcs-evaluate-risk-when-you-pitch/
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/
  https://a16z.com/the-16-commandments-of-raising-equity-in-a-challenging-market/

- The tone should be more investor-ready: less "here is our vision and workflow" and more "here is the opportunity, here is why we win, here is how risk is managed, and here is what this capital or commitment unlocks."
  Sources:
  https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck
  https://sequoiacap.com/article/writing-a-business-plan/
  https://review.firstround.com/the-fundraising-wisdom-that-helped-our-founders-raise-18b-in-follow-on-capital/

- Because this project may seek money, time, or commitment from different audiences, the safest structure is probably one shared core narrative with audience-specific variants:
  - Investor/backer variant: opportunity, moat, economics, risk, ask
  - Contributor/community variant: impact, roadmap, governance, contribution leverage, sponsor value
  This is an inference from the combined investor and open-source/community research.
  Sources informing this inference:
  https://opensource.guide/getting-paid/
  https://opensource.guide/best-practices/
  https://github.blog/open-source/maintainers/4-trends-shaping-open-source-funding-and-what-they-mean-for-maintainers/
