# Executive Roadmap Communication Research

## What Executives Need to See

- Executives want a roadmap that is a strategic communication tool, not a task tracker. Repeated guidance across Atlassian, Productboard, and ProductPlan is to show vision, direction, priorities, progress, and the rationale behind choices rather than detailed delivery plans.  
  Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/product-roadmaps, https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/

- The roadmap should be tailored to the audience. Executive views should emphasize company goals, KPIs, portfolio context, and major initiatives, while detailed task plans belong to delivery teams.  
  Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/product-roadmaps, https://www.productplan.com/learn/5-roadmap-templates-product-executives/, https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/

- High-level formats work better than feature dumps. Theme-based, objective-led, OKR-led, portfolio, and now-next-later views recur as executive-friendly because they keep attention on why the company is investing, not on implementation minutiae.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/5-roadmap-templates-product-executives/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/, https://productschool.com/blog/product-strategy/outcome-based-roadmap

- The right detail level is directional, not exhaustive. ProductPlan explicitly recommends minimal text, approximate timing, and a short forward view; Atlassian similarly argues for clarity on the big picture with only enough detail to drive decisions and action.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/product-roadmaps

- Roadmaps for executives should connect directly to measurable business outcomes. Outcome-based roadmaps, OKR roadmaps, and objective-led roadmaps are all framed as better executive communication because they tie work to growth, retention, revenue, churn, or other business results.  
  Sources: https://productschool.com/blog/product-strategy/outcome-based-roadmap, https://www.productplan.com/learn/5-roadmap-templates-product-executives/, https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/

- Vision must be paired with execution realism. Atlassian warns against buzzwords, unrealistic commitments, and plans that ignore capacity constraints; BCG recommends an integrated narrative that ties ambition to priorities, initiatives, operational changes, and a roadmap for value creation.  
  Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/tips-for-presenting-product-roadmaps, https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days

- Executives also need to understand what is not being done. BCG recommends clarity on the paths the company will not take, while ProductPlan advises proactively addressing likely objections and controversial tradeoffs.  
  Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/

## Risk Communication

- Risks should be surfaced early and regularly, not saved for crises. Diligent recommends proactive communication, timely disclosure, and complete and accurate information even when the news is negative. ProductPlan likewise advises not to hide bad news from boards or investors.  
  Sources: https://www.diligent.com/resources/blog/effective-board-communication, https://www.productplan.com/learn/presenting-product-roadmap-to-investors/

- The executive posture on risk is not “avoid mentioning risk,” but “show that risk is understood, bounded, and managed.” Gartner frames executive communication around business outcomes, defensible investment choices, and explicit tradeoffs between protection and spend.  
  Sources: https://www.gartner.com/en/cybersecurity/insights/communicate-risk-and-value-to-the-board

- The risk categories executives consistently care about include timing and interdependencies, funding and capital constraints, capability and talent bottlenecks, technology capacity, and leadership bandwidth. BCG lays these out as the main constraints that should reshape sequencing decisions.  
  Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/the-power-of-priorities-in-a-transformation

- Communication should show risk in context of sequencing. BCG’s prioritization guidance is explicit that leaders should gather constraints, categorize initiatives, and sequence work deliberately so scarce resources are applied to the highest-value items first.  
  Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/the-power-of-priorities-in-a-transformation

- Executives respond better to risks that come paired with mitigation, ownership, and discussion structure. Diligent recommends pre-reading, open dialogue, committee ownership for deep dives, concise management reports, and dashboard views that make risk discussion actionable instead of abstract.  
  Sources: https://www.diligent.com/resources/blog/effective-board-communication

- Communicating certainty levels is a practical risk-control device. ProductPlan recommends explicitly labeling roadmap confidence so executives can distinguish current commitments from lower-certainty future plans.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/

## Cost and Investment Communication

- Executives want investments framed as value creation, not just spend. BCG recommends a financial and transformation roadmap that lays out required investments, revenue and cost projections, operational improvements, and the speed and shape of value creation over time.  
  Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days

- Boards and investors view roadmap choices as a portfolio of bets. ProductPlan explicitly advises presenters to think like investors and explain where investments are being made and how they are expected to pay off in the next 6, 12, and 24 months.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/presenting-product-roadmap-to-investors/

- Resource requirements should be visible enough to explain feasibility. Productboard says strong roadmaps include the resources involved, including people, tools, and time. Atlassian adds that prioritization should account for effort constraints and available resources.  
  Sources: https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/product-roadmaps, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework

- Investment cases should stay outcome-linked. Gartner argues that executive decisions are stronger when investments are tied to specific business requirements and measurable outcomes rather than abstract “importance.”  
  Sources: https://www.gartner.com/en/cybersecurity/insights/communicate-risk-and-value-to-the-board

- Uncertainty should be acknowledged structurally, not apologetically. BCG recommends upside and downside scenarios to show likely capital allocation and cash implications. ProductPlan recommends confidence bands, and Atlassian recommends being open about unknowns, open questions, and experimentation time.  
  Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/tips-for-presenting-product-roadmaps

- Prioritization frameworks help justify investment logic, but they are supporting evidence rather than the main story. Intercom’s RICE and Atlassian’s overview of RICE, MoSCoW, value vs. effort, opportunity scoring, Kano, and cost of delay all position frameworks as ways to make tradeoffs explicit and defensible.  
  Sources: https://www.intercom.com/blog/rice-simple-prioritization-for-product-managers/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework

## Competitive Positioning

- Executives expect the roadmap to show how the product wins, not merely what it ships. Productboard recommends including target market, market trends, competitive intelligence, and unique differentiators. BCG likewise says leaders should be clear-eyed about assets, genuine differentiation, and which rivals require defense versus offense.  
  Sources: https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/, https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days

- Competitive framing should be confident and specific, not defensive. BCG’s strategy palette argues that strategy depends on environment: in stable markets advantage comes from scale, differentiation, or capabilities; in unpredictable markets it comes from speed and adaptation; in nascent markets it can come from being first or orchestrating the ecosystem.  
  Sources: https://www.bcg.com/capabilities/corporate-finance-strategy/strategy-palette

- The roadmap should explain why now. Productboard points to milestone-driven planning around conferences, launches, partner commitments, and similar forcing functions. ProductPlan and Productboard both suggest that time buckets and near-term horizons keep plans tied to market reality rather than speculative long-range promises.  
  Sources: https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/

- Competitive positioning is stronger when tied to outcomes customers and executives care about. Outcome-based roadmap guidance repeatedly recommends describing the intended impact first, then showing how initiatives support that impact.  
  Sources: https://productschool.com/blog/product-strategy/outcome-based-roadmap, https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/

- Inference from BCG and Productboard: the most persuasive competitive section of an executive roadmap is a short argument that links market pressure, current differentiation, and the sequence of initiatives required to widen that differentiation before the window closes.  
  Sources informing this inference: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days, https://www.bcg.com/capabilities/corporate-finance-strategy/strategy-palette, https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/

## Writing Style

- The tone that works is concise, evidence-based, and confident without pretending certainty that does not exist. ProductPlan says to keep it short and sweet, Atlassian says to choose substance over buzzwords, and Diligent emphasizes complete and unbiased information.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/presenting-product-roadmap-to-investors/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/tips-for-presenting-product-roadmaps, https://www.diligent.com/resources/blog/effective-board-communication

- Executives prefer memo-like communication over dense decks. ProductPlan explicitly says to think of the presentation as a memo, not a novel.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/presenting-product-roadmap-to-investors/

- Good executive communication leads with rationale. Atlassian repeatedly emphasizes the importance of context, evidence, user story, and business case so audiences understand why the roadmap exists and why these choices are being made now.  
  Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/tips-for-presenting-product-roadmaps

- Effective documents are short in the core narrative and deep in backup material only when needed. ProductPlan recommends brief main materials with backup slides ready for objections; Diligent recommends pre-meeting materials, summaries, dashboards, and committee deep dives.  
  Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.diligent.com/resources/blog/effective-board-communication

- Inference from ProductPlan, Diligent, and BCG: the strongest structure is a brief executive summary up front, a few focused sections on strategy, sequencing, investment, and risk, and an appendix for methods, data, and deeper evidence.  
  Sources informing this inference: https://www.productplan.com/learn/presenting-product-roadmap-to-investors/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.diligent.com/resources/blog/effective-board-communication, https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days

- Common communication mistakes are consistent across sources: feature-heavy roadmaps with no “why,” hard date commitments beyond confidence, unrealistic plans that ignore capacity, hiding bad news, using buzzwords instead of evidence, and letting the roadmap go stale.  
  Sources: https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/tips-for-presenting-product-roadmaps, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/product-roadmaps

## Frameworks and Examples

- Common roadmap formats for executives:
  - Theme-based roadmap for strategic narrative and flexibility.  
    Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/5-roadmap-templates-product-executives/
  - Objective or OKR-led roadmap for outcome accountability.  
    Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/5-roadmap-templates-product-executives/, https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/, https://productschool.com/blog/product-strategy/outcome-based-roadmap
  - Portfolio roadmap for multi-product or multi-initiative executive visibility.  
    Sources: https://www.productplan.com/learn/5-roadmap-templates-product-executives/, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/
  - Now-next-later or quarterly buckets for uncertain environments.  
    Sources: https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/, https://productschool.com/blog/product-strategy/outcome-based-roadmap

- Common prioritization frameworks used to justify roadmap choices:
  - `RICE`: reach, impact, confidence, effort.  
    Sources: https://www.intercom.com/blog/rice-simple-prioritization-for-product-managers/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework
  - `MoSCoW`: must, should, could, will not.  
    Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework
  - `Value vs. effort`: visual tradeoff matrix for quick wins vs. expensive low-value work.  
    Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework
  - `Cost of delay`: prioritization by economic cost of waiting.  
    Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework
  - `Kano` and `opportunity scoring`: customer-value-oriented support methods.  
    Sources: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework

- BCG contributes two useful executive frameworks that are less “product” and more board-grade:
  - A prioritization method of gather, evaluate, categorize, and sequence.  
    Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/the-power-of-priorities-in-a-transformation
  - An executive roadmap package of strategic narrative, financial and transformation roadmap, stakeholder plan, communications plan, and talent/readiness assessment.  
    Sources: https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/impact-of-ceo-first-100-days

- Productboard’s examples are useful because they distinguish leadership roadmaps, customer-facing roadmaps, and delivery roadmaps, reinforcing that “one roadmap for everyone” is a communication failure mode.  
  Sources: https://www.productboard.com/blog/7-product-roadmap-examples/, https://www.productboard.com/product-roadmap-guide/

- Inference from the framework set above: executives rarely need to see the scoring mechanics in the main narrative. They need to know that prioritization was disciplined, what the decision criteria were, and what tradeoffs the criteria produced. Detailed scoring tables belong in an appendix.  
  Sources informing this inference: https://www.intercom.com/blog/rice-simple-prioritization-for-product-managers/, https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/prioritization-framework, https://www.productplan.com/learn/executive-roadmap/

## Synthesis: Requirements for Our Executive Roadmap

Based on the research, our executive roadmap document should add the communication elements below.

1. Start with an executive summary that answers five questions immediately:
   - What strategic outcome are we trying to create?
   - Why is this the right sequence now?
   - What approval or commitment is being requested?
   - What value should stakeholders expect, and when?
   - What are the top risks and how are they being managed?

2. Reframe the roadmap around outcomes and strategic themes, not just delivery phases.
   - The existing value slices and phases are useful, but each phase should explicitly state the business outcome, the user/community pain being relieved, and the metric or qualitative condition that proves success.

3. Add a portfolio-level narrative.
   - Explain how the four phases fit together into one coherent story of competitive positioning, operational leverage, and risk reduction.
   - State what the roadmap deliberately does not pursue yet.

4. Add an investment view for each initiative or phase.
   - Required resources or capability ask.
   - Major dependencies.
   - Value realization window such as near-term, 6 months, 12 months, or longer.
   - Confidence level or certainty band.

5. Add explicit risk framing.
   - Risk category.
   - Why the risk matters to executives.
   - Mitigation or sequencing decision that contains it.
   - Trigger or indicator that tells leadership the risk is increasing.
   - Owner, if the roadmap is intended to become an operating document.

6. Add a competitive positioning section.
   - What alternatives communities use today.
   - Where the current market is weak.
   - What distinctive advantage this sequence creates for Humility’s platform.
   - Why the timing matters now rather than later.

7. Make the writing style more executive-grade.
   - Short, factual, and directional.
   - Minimal feature detail in the core document.
   - Clear tradeoff language.
   - No hype language, no vague transformation rhetoric, no unexplained jargon.

8. Use a layered document structure.
   - Core document: executive summary, strategic narrative, roadmap by phase, investment view, risk view, approval ask.
   - Appendix: scoring framework, deeper assumptions, dependency detail, detailed estimates, competitor notes, backup evidence.

9. End with a concrete approval package.
   - What leadership is approving.
   - What remains flexible.
   - What review cadence will be used to revisit the roadmap.

Inference: for this project, the strongest format is likely a theme-and-phase hybrid. Keep the four phases, but make each phase read as an executive investment thesis with explicit outcome, differentiation effect, investment requirement, risk posture, and opportunity cost. That preserves the current structure while adding the decision-oriented communication executives expect.
